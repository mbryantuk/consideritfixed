# Consider IT Fixed Branding & Style Guide

## 1. Brand Identity & Tone of Voice
- **Identity:** Local, reliable, neighborly, and expert. You are the friendly IT professional next door in Felpham, West Sussex.
- **Tone:** 
  - **Empathetic & Reassuring:** Acknowledge that technology can be frustrating. Avoid making the user feel inadequate.
  - **Clear & Simple:** Absolutely no unnecessary technical jargon. If a technical term must be used, explain it simply. (e.g., instead of "SSID configuration," use "Setting up your Wi-Fi name and password").
  - **Professional but not Corporate:** Avoid stiff, overly formal language. Write as if you are speaking to a customer in their living room. 
  - **Trustworthy (Anti-Scam):** Be highly transparent about costs, processes, and identity. Avoid urgent, aggressive sales tactics ("Call NOW!", flashing banners).

## 2. Color Palette
The colors must inspire trust, security, and calmness, while maintaining strict high-contrast ratios for accessibility (WCAG AA/AAA).

- **Primary Brand Color (Trust & Security):** Deep Navy Blue (`#0F172A` or `#1E3A8A`). Used for main headers, primary buttons, and the footer.
- **Secondary/Accent Color (Friendly & Action-Oriented):** Soft Teal (`#0D9488`) or a Warm, muted Orange (`#EA580C`). Used sparingly for call-to-action buttons (like "Get a Quote") to make them stand out without looking alarming.
- **Backgrounds:** 
  - Main Body: Pure White (`#FFFFFF`) or Off-White (`#F8FAFC`) for maximum readability.
  - Secondary Sections (like alternating rows): Very Light Gray (`#F1F5F9`).
- **Text:** 
  - Primary Text: Dark Slate (`#334155`) — easier on the eyes than pure black.
  - Muted Text: Gray (`#64748B`) — for secondary information or timestamps.

## 3. Typography
Focus entirely on legibility for users with poor eyesight or reading difficulties.

- **Primary Typeface:** A highly legible, modern sans-serif font like **Inter**, **Roboto**, or **Open Sans**.
- **Sizing:** 
  - Base font size should be large: `18px` or `1.125rem` minimum for body text.
  - Generous line height (e.g., `1.6` to `1.8`) to make paragraphs easy to read.
- **Hierarchy:** Clear distinction between Headings (H1, H2, H3) and paragraph text using weight and size, not just color.

## 4. UI/UX Style Guidelines
- **Buttons & Touch Targets:** Large, padded buttons (minimum `44x44px` for mobile touch). Slightly rounded corners (e.g., `border-radius: 8px`) to look friendly and approachable, avoiding harsh, sharp edges.
- **Whitespace:** Use plenty of padding and margin (negative space). Do not cram information together. A spacious layout feels calmer and less overwhelming to a stressed user.
- **Forms & Inputs:** 
  - Inputs must have clear, highly visible borders.
  - Labels must always be visible outside the input (do not rely solely on placeholders, which disappear when typing).
  - Provide immediate, clear feedback (e.g., "Email sent!" in a green box, rather than a generic pop-up).
- **Imagery:** Use authentic-looking, high-quality photos (ideally real photos of you/your business eventually, but high-quality, non-cheesy stock photos of friendly people helping with technology for now). Avoid abstract, overly "cyber" graphics that might feel alienating or scammy.
